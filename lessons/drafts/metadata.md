### 2.a Reading descriptions of historical data: the mediation of the archive
    
The search of <!-- this would rather be searching _for_ sources, no? --> historical sources for the purpose of a given research involves operations of discovery and localizing for accessing, and eventually using (i.e. make a digital or other copy, take notes, etc to be able to analyze) historical materials of interest. An essential factor for discovering these materials, be they born-digital, digitized, or of any analog materiality, is their description in dedicated catalogs, that function as finding aids.

Depending on the context, these descriptions may use norms, i.e. rules agreed by professional communities such as archivists and librarians, or they can be more flexible functional systems at the level of a community, team or an individual. In either case, the aim is to establish intelligible and stable ways to find one's way around a plethora of resources, something like when we need to pick our way among autoroutes, and we use road signs that many recognize to signify the same things. <!-- you can refer back to my part with _decriptive categories_ here -->

In web environments, discoverability can additionally be ensured by what we call full indexation of the content of textual resources - picture this as if each word of a text was underlined to make it evident. In this way, (parts of) content become findable regardless theor global contect, even if the descriptive information for the whole text is absent or not systematic (with the inherent risk of having fragmented information outside of its original context of creation/classification, and for this reason subject to caution to analyze and interpret, but we will not focus on this at this point!). <!-- the entire preceding paragraph needs to be re-worked, IMO. The abundance of historical primary sources that are digitally available and accessible on the web opened the debate among historians about visibility and consequent prominence of digitized materials versus a, sometimes perceived as silent, non-digitized cultural heritage (Putnam 2016, Milligan 2013). However, not all historical records can be digitally available, and the majority of the holdings of the archives and libraries is <!-- _are_ --> still not. Nevertheless, what is important is to somehow find out that these records exist, when we look for them. Which is why digital availability of descriptive information on the historical records, independently from their materiality, is precious (Zaagsma 2022). In other words, what we call with the fancy name ["metadata"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metadata) is essential for historians to be able to discover, localize, and contextualize their sources in order to analyze and interpret them, which is an essential part of the work they are supposed to do.

### 2.c Standards, folks, and individuals

Things that hold information content (texts, videos, images, publications on online social networks...) can be described in standardized ways, or in more loose ways, depending the context they are created and stored, and the finality of their use. Standardized ways are typically met in GLAM's catalogs that describe collections. What are these? Well, they consist of what we call controlled vocabularies: lists of terms and keywords that are agreed in a professional community as an objective way to describe their resources. Who does so? Usually, it is professional bodies, for example associations of librarians, archivists, etc, that define standards at a supra level (national, international...). Why do this? The aim is to achieve what we call [interoperabilty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interoperability), i.e. communications of the information systems, and effective organisation of the collections in a way that enhances they way they are explored and interrogated by users. How does this work? Let's focus on [indexation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subject_indexing) keywords that help organize and classify information objects.

Keywords in online social networks configurations are called tags, a term that was popularized parallel to the possibility of users to easily generate content themselves on online social networks or customized websites and blogs. [A tag is one or a set of textual keywords that does not follow any formal guidelines. It defines a relationship between an online resource and a concept in the user's mind.](https://mirror.dlib.org/dlib/january06/guy/01guy.html) Thus, it is by definition subjective and customized, born to make sense in personal, rather than universal, contexts. Any number of words might be chosen, some of which are obvious representations, others making less sense outside the tag author's context.

[Tags are the basis of folksonomies; e.g. classification systems that are created by individuals to tag resources of the social web, i.e. photos, texts, videos, music etc on online social networks and platforms.](https://mirror.dlib.org/dlib/january06/guy/01guy.html). Folksonomies are thus decentralized and non authoritative systems of classification of shared information on the web.
