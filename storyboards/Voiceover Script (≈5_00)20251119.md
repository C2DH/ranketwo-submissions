> **Opening (0:00–0:20)**
> A map doesn’t just show space; it **interprets** it. Every map draws on the knowledge and technologies of its time. What if the maps we use weren’t neutral windows on the world, but crafted statements about it?
> In this lesson, we’ll treat maps as **abstractions of reality** and as **products of their makers, circumstances, and time**—useful, powerful, and open to challenge.
> ✂︎ *Remove either sentence one or two if you need –10s total across the script.*

**Principle 1 — Maps are products of their makers (0:21–0:55)**
No map floats free of context. A map carries its maker’s tools, purposes, and constraints: the information available, the intended audience, the politics of the moment, even printing or platform limits.
When we study a map, we study the people and institutions behind it—who they were, what they valued, and what they needed the map to do.

**Principle 2 — Maps are reductions of reality (0:56–1:25)**
A map is never the territory; a perfect copy of space is impossible. To be readable, a map must simplify—generalizing shapes, omitting details, smoothing lines, and fixing a moving, three-dimensional world onto a flat surface.
That reduction is a feature, not a flaw—but the **omissions matter**.

**Principle 3 — Every map is a series of choices (1:26–2:05)**
You start with an infinite reality—you can’t show everything—so you **choose**. Which features matter? Rivers? Footpaths? Borders—and from which year?
All maps are built from choices: which **data** to trust; which **categories** to use; which **colors** to assign; what **symbols**, **labels**, and **projections** to adopt; where to place **focus** and what to push back.
Those choices guide interpretation: they can clarify patterns—or create them. Historians name the choices and ask how they shape the story the map tells.

**Principle 4 — Mapping is a powerful act (2:06–2:40)**
To map is to **argue**. Mapping makes claims about where people live, who belongs, what resources lie where, and which routes matter. The choice of colors and where lines are drawn affects our view on borders, influences political decisions and directs aid. Anyone can map. But whose map is read has the power to influence perspectives—so mapping carries responsibility.

**Principle 5 — Maps are always political (2:41–3:10)**
“Political” here means maps participate in decisions about **power, inclusion, and visibility**. Boundaries, place names, data sources, and legends encode viewpoints.
Seeing the politics helps us grasp what a map makes visible, what it leaves out, and **for what purpose**. Who gets to map? Whose knowledge counts? What stories have official maps erased?

**Principle 6 — Maps can and should be challenged (3:11–3:40)**
Like any source, maps invite **critical analysis**. We compare editions, read titles and metadata, check sources, and set maps beside other media—photographs, texts, oral histories.
Sometimes we answer a map with a **counter-map** that centers another community, boundary, or category. Consider a map of the Mediterranean without borders—or a south-up world map. There’s no rule that north must be up; that’s just a convention we rarely question.
✂︎ *If you’re tight on time, keep only the south-up example (–15s).*

**Principle 7 — Digital tools: more possibilities (3:41–4:05)**
Digital tools have **democratized mapping**. Anyone can build layers with GIS, challenge narratives, and publish spatial arguments—even in real time.
For historians, that means an abundance of sources—often minimally mediated and available on the live web—so critical evaluation is essential.

 **Principle 8 — Platforms mediate access (repositories) (4:06–4:25)**
Dedicated digital archives and map libraries make historical maps easier to find, but each platform mediates what you see. Scan quality varies. Metadata may be rich—or sparse. Licenses can invite reuse or restrict it.
Check **creator, purpose, scope, and rights**. Good metadata speeds research and lets you trace origins; poor metadata slows you down—or misleads.

 **Principle 9 — GIS is not cartography (4:26–4:50)**
**GIS** is a toolkit for **building and analyzing layers** on a georeferenced base; **cartography** is the craft of designing a legible, persuasive map.
Imagine transparent sheets: population density, trade routes, disease outbreaks, battle sites. Turn layers on and off, combine them, and ask spatial questions. (For example, the WWII Luxembourg spatio-temporal layers in the lesson reveal how space shaped events.)

**Principle 10 — Study maps like any source (4:51–5:00)**
Ask: Who made it, when, and for whom? What choices structure it? What does it make visible—and what does it hide? How does it compare with other sources? And don't forget to critically interrogate the digital infrastructures that shape what you can see and analyze.
